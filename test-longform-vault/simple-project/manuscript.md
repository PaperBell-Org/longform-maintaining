abstract: "Water quotas are increasingly used to address water scarcity in cross-boundary river basins worldwide, but whether they improve sustainability or merely shift pressure across water sources remains unclear. Here, we modelled 59 prefecture-level water agencies along China's Yellow River and their irrigation withdrawal responses to a top-down water quota policy. We coupled an agent-based model, in which agencies trade off economic returns against institutional compliance, with a process-based crop growth module to capture heterogeneous behavioural responses to quota constraints. By comparing the historical scenario with counterfactual experiments, we show that the 1998 institutional strengthening of basin-wide quota enforcement increased surface-water productivity (+32.8%) and irrigation efficiency (+6.3%). However, it also increased groundwater reliance by ~19%, consistent with observed aquifer depletion. Under a stricter enforcement counterfactual, groundwater substitution would have been even greater (+35.8%). These findings demonstrate that water quotas can improve irrigation performance while undermining groundwater sustainability through risk-shifting behaviours—a pattern likely relevant to quota-based governance in other major basins globally. Our model highlights the need for integrated surface-water and groundwater governance, alongside enhanced metering and monitoring, and provides a transferable framework for anticipating policy-induced trade-offs."
